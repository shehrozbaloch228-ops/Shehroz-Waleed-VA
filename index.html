Case Studies / Yield Profiles

Dense operating histories presented as measurable yield outcomes.

These profiles frame the work like a high-end agency case book: the original state, the operational move set, and the realized yield outcome.

Yield Profile 01 Recovered $42K

Stranded inventory recovery and fee leakage cleanup.

Initial State

Large shipments were arriving into FBA with partial discrepancies, stranded listings, and inconsistent reimbursement follow-up.

Execution

Audited shipment events, built a claim log, prioritized recovery by dollar impact, and tracked every open case until collection.

Yield Outcome

Recovered $42K in stranded inventory and fee leakage while improving the accuracy of the operational reimbursement cadence.

Yield Profile 02 Optimized Rhythm

FBA replenishment rhythm and cash efficiency.

Initial State

Reorder timing was reactive, causing occasional stock pressure and excess capital sitting in slow-moving inventory.

Execution

Built a replenishment cadence from sell-through, lead times, and buffer inventory; aligned purchasing to margin reality and cash flow.

Yield Outcome

Optimized FBA replenishment rhythm, lowered emergency purchasing, and stabilized availability without overbuying inventory.

Yield Profile 03 Margin Defended

Buy-box protection and competitive margin defense.

Initial State

Pricing pressure and competitor reshuffles were compressing contribution margin on key replenishment SKUs.

Execution

Monitored competitive intervals, adjusted price floors, validated profitability thresholds, and tightened escalation windows.

Yield Outcome

Protected margin integrity on the core portfolio while keeping the offer competitive enough to maintain sell-through velocity.

Operational FAQ

Technical questions for disciplined wholesale operations.

Each answer expands dynamically so the section feels immediate, precise, and calm under interaction.

I use price floors, contribution margin thresholds, and competitive windows to avoid reactive discounting. The goal is to preserve the unit economics while maintaining enough competitiveness for buy-box retention.
I keep rolling records for replenishment, reimbursement, buy-box events, and margin changes. That creates a clear audit trail and makes it easier to explain every decision with evidence rather than assumptions.
I tie reorder points to sell-through velocity, lead times, and safety stock assumptions, then review them weekly. That lets me keep the supply chain agile while limiting excess working capital in slow-moving units.
The split depends on velocity, storage economics, and fulfillment reliability. I use FBA for speed-sensitive products and FBM as a risk buffer where cost, inventory exposure, or timing discipline requires more control.
I rank claims by recoverable value, age, and confidence of documentation. High-value, high-confidence claims are processed first because they restore capital fastest and strengthen operational discipline.
